Uniform MLE Explorer — Quant Story

We observe realized P&L from a market-neutral strategy. The purpose is to estimate the hidden regime parameter that determines the lower and upper support of tomorrow’s edge.

Ahmed Alzahrani
1.

The Quant Story (analytical motivation)

A market-neutral strategy is designed with risk limits. On a comparable trading window, the P&L cannot fall below a practical worst-case loss and cannot exceed a practical best-case gain.

Those limits are not directly observed. We only see realized outcomes. If the strategy environment becomes more favorable or less favorable, both the downside and upside limits may shift.

The analytical task is therefore simple: from observed P&L values, infer the most plausible hidden market-pressure level that makes those outcomes possible with the tightest support.

Goal: Estimate the latent regime parameter that determines the worst plausible edge and best plausible edge for the strategy.
2.

Model Setup: What We Know vs. What We Estimate

What we know / assume
  • Realized edge is bounded inside a support interval.
  • Within that interval, outcomes are treated as equally likely.
  • Comparable windows are modeled as i.i.d.
  • A latent regime parameter shifts both endpoints linearly.
What we need to estimate
  • The hidden market-pressure parameter \(\theta\).
  • The lower endpoint \(a\theta+b\), interpreted as worst plausible edge.
  • The upper endpoint \(c\theta+d\), interpreted as best plausible edge.
\[ X_i\sim \operatorname{Unif}(a\theta+b,\,c\theta+d), \qquad f(x_i\mid\theta)= \frac{1}{(c-a)\theta+(d-b)}\, \mathbf 1\{a\theta+b\le x_i\le c\theta+d\}. \]

Likelihood Function and Full MLE Derivation

For i.i.d. data from the model above, the likelihood is the product of the uniform densities. Since only the sample minimum and maximum determine whether the support contains all observations, write

\[ x_{(1)}:=\min\{x_1,\ldots,x_n\}, \qquad x_{(n)}:=\max\{x_1,\ldots,x_n\}. \]

Likelihood in support-constrained form

\[ L(\theta;x)= \begin{cases} \big((c-a)\theta+(d-b)\big)^{-n}, & a\theta+b\le x_{(1)}\le x_{(n)}\le c\theta+d,\\[4pt] 0, & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} \]

The exponent \(-n\) comes from multiplying \(n\) identical uniform densities. It does not change which boundary maximizes the likelihood; it only changes the vertical scale.

The following cases are exhaustive.

  1. Suppose \(a=c=0\) and \(b In this case \[ L(\theta;x)= \begin{cases} (d-b)^{-n}, & b\le x_{(1)}\le x_{(n)}\le d,\\[4pt] 0, & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} \] Therefore, the likelihood does not depend on \(\theta\). Therefore \[ \boxed{\hat\theta\in(-\infty,+\infty)}. \]
  2. Suppose \(a>0\) and \(c=0\). In this case \[ \ell(\theta;x)=\ln L(\theta;x) = \begin{cases} -n\ln\big(-a\theta+(d-b)\big), & -\infty<\theta\le \dfrac{x_{(1)}-b}{a},\\[8pt] -\infty, & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} \] Taking the derivative with respect to \(\theta\), over the positive-likelihood interval, \[ \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\ell(\theta;x)= \frac{na}{-a\theta+(d-b)}>0. \] Therefore, the likelihood is strictly monotonically increasing on the positive-likelihood interval. Therefore, \[ \boxed{\hat\theta=\frac{x_{(1)}-b}{a}}. \]
  3. Similarly, if \(a=0\) and \(c>0\), then \[ \boxed{\hat\theta=\frac{x_{(n)}-d}{c}}. \]
  4. Suppose \(c-a=0\). In this case \[ L(\theta;x)= \begin{cases} (d-b)^{-n}, & a\theta+b\le x_{(1)}\le x_{(n)}\le c\theta+d,\\[4pt] 0, & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} \] Equivalently, \[ L(\theta;x)= \begin{cases} (d-b)^{-n}, & \dfrac{x_{(n)}-d}{c}\le \theta\le \dfrac{x_{(1)}-b}{a},\\[8pt] 0, & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} \] Therefore, the likelihood is constant over positive-likelihood parameters. Therefore, \[ \boxed{\hat\theta\in\left[\frac{x_{(n)}-d}{c},\frac{x_{(1)}-b}{a}\right]}. \]
  5. Suppose \(c-a\ne 0\). In this case \[ L(\theta;x)= \begin{cases} \big((c-a)\theta+(d-b)\big)^{-n}, & \dfrac{x_{(n)}-d}{c}\le \theta\le \dfrac{x_{(1)}-b}{a},\\[8pt] 0, & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} \] The log-likelihood is then \[ \ell(\theta;x)= \begin{cases} -n\ln\big((c-a)\theta+(d-b)\big), & \dfrac{x_{(n)}-d}{c}\le \theta\le \dfrac{x_{(1)}-b}{a},\\[8pt] -\infty, & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} \] Taking the derivative with respect to \(\theta\), \[ \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\ell(\theta;x)= \frac{-n(c-a)}{(c-a)\theta+(d-b)}. \]
    • If \(c-a>0\), then over positive-likelihood parameters, \(\ell'(\theta)<0\), hence the likelihood is strictly decreasing; therefore, \[ \boxed{\hat\theta=\frac{x_{(n)}-d}{c}}. \]
    • If \(c-a<0\), then over positive-likelihood parameters, \(\ell'(\theta)>0\), hence the likelihood is strictly increasing; therefore, \[ \boxed{\hat\theta=\frac{x_{(1)}-b}{a}}. \]
The structural lesson is that this is a boundary MLE. The derivative decides whether the likelihood climbs toward the left feasible endpoint or the right feasible endpoint.

Why the Result Matches the Charts

Feasible interval

\[ a\theta+b\le x_{(1)}, \qquad x_{(n)}\le c\theta+d. \] When \(a>0\) and \(c>0\), this becomes \[ \frac{x_{(n)}-d}{c}\le \theta\le \frac{x_{(1)}-b}{a}. \]

Width rule

\[ w(\theta)=c\theta+d-(a\theta+b) =(c-a)\theta+(d-b). \] Because \(L(\theta;x)=w(\theta)^{-n}\) on the feasible set, maximizing likelihood is the same as minimizing \(w(\theta)\).

Case: \(c>a\)

\[ w'(\theta)=c-a>0. \] Width increases with \(\theta\), so choose the smallest feasible \(\theta\): \[ \hat\theta=\frac{x_{(n)}-d}{c}. \]

Case: \(c<a\)

\[ w'(\theta)=c-a<0. \] Width decreases with \(\theta\), so choose the largest feasible \(\theta\): \[ \hat\theta=\frac{x_{(1)}-b}{a}. \]
8.

MLE Invariance

Once \(\hat\theta\) is known, every deterministic downstream trading quantity is estimated by substitution. This is the MLE invariance principle: if \(\hat\theta\) maximizes the likelihood for \(\theta\), then \(g(\hat\theta)\) is the MLE of \(g(\theta)\).

\[ \widehat{g(\theta)} = g(\hat\theta). \] No new optimization is needed. Once the endpoint model gives \(\hat\theta\), estimated mean edge, upside quantiles, hit probabilities, and support width are all obtained by direct substitution.

Deriving the downstream formulas

Once \(\hat\theta\) is found, define \[ \hat\ell=a\hat\theta+b, \qquad \hat u=c\hat\theta+d, \qquad \hat w=\hat u-\hat\ell=(c-a)\hat\theta+(d-b). \] For \(X\sim\operatorname{Unif}(\hat\ell,\hat u)\), \[ \widehat{\mathbb E[X]}=\frac{\hat\ell+\hat u}{2} =\frac{(a\hat\theta+b)+(c\hat\theta+d)}{2}, \] \[ \widehat{Q}_{0.95}=\hat\ell+0.95\hat w =a\hat{\theta}+b+0.95\big((c-a)\hat{\theta}+(d-b)\big), \] and the hit probability for positive P&L is the length of the support above zero divided by the total support width: \[ \widehat{\mathbb P}(X>0)= \begin{cases} 1, & \hat\ell\ge 0,\\[3pt] 0, & \hat u\le 0,\\[3pt] \dfrac{\hat u}{\hat w}, & \hat\ell<0<\hat u. \end{cases} \]

In quant language: the likelihood estimates the hidden support parameter, and invariance pushes that estimate through the trading quantity you actually care about. That is why a boundary MLE is useful even when the final object is not itself a boundary.
